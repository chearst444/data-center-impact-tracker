Community Stewardship Toolkit

The true, long-term cost of an industrial data center on your town.

An interactive, data-driven civic blueprint for residents, municipal leaders, and pastors. The Tracker moves past the marketing brochure to surface what arrives quietly after the ribbon-cutting: redistributed electric bills, summer water competition, 24/7 low-frequency hum, diesel exhaust, NDA-shielded deals, stranded concrete — and what to do about all of it.

Module 06

The "Secret Deals" Transparency & NDA Tracker

"Project Bluebird." "Olive Branch Holdings." The signature move of the hyperscale playbook is the placeholder LLC.

Feature: A civic alert tool that flags the use of Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs) by city officials.

  • Shell companies and NDAsDevelopers commonly use placeholder LLCs and strict NDAs to hide project scale, water needs, and power needs from the public until the day of the vote.
  • Compressed comment windowsBy the time the project is unveiled, public comment is often measured in days, not months.
  • The remedy is proceduralDisclosure must be required before the rezoning vote, not after.

"Right to Know" Citizen Checklist

  • Disclose the actual parent company behind any LLC or shell entity applying for zoning, annexation, or utility service.
  • Publish the projected continuous and peak megawatt load before any rezoning vote.
  • Publish the projected daily and peak water demand, in gallons, with seasonal breakdowns.
  • Disclose all NDAs signed by any city or county official, staff member, or board.
  • Allow a 60-day public comment window before the rezoning vote.
  • Require a signed sound-budget commitment with a verifiable nighttime dB(A) cap at the residential property line.
  • Require a signed water-curtailment plan with binding drought triggers.
  • Require a developer commitment that residential ratepayers do not bear dedicated transmission, substation, and water-treatment costs.

Module 08

The "Bullying Lawsuit" Legal Defense Repository

A small board's hands are tied after the zoning matrix is left ambiguous — not before.

Feature: A legal resource database for small-town boards whose "hands are tied" by corporate litigation.

  • Litigation pressure patternSmall townships that vote "No" can face multi-million-dollar suits engineered to force settlements.
  • Best defense is proceduralPre-existing ordinances that treat data centers as a distinct land use are the single most effective shield.
  • Ratepayer-protection clausesBind the developer to fund 100% of dedicated infrastructure — closing the silent-subsidy door.

Defensive drafting principles

  • Define hyperscale data centers as a distinct use — not "general industrial."
  • Require special-use permits, not by-right approvals.
  • Adopt water-availability and sound-budget findings as conditions precedent.
  • Include a ratepayer-protection clause for developer-funded infrastructure.
  • Bind tax abatement to permanent jobs-to-MW thresholds with claw-backs.

Pro-Bono Style Resources

A starter model ordinance defining "Hyperscale Data Center" as a distinct use, requiring special-use permit, water-availability finding, sound-budget, and ratepayer-protection clause. Adapt with local counsel.

Module 11

The Scriptural Reference & Stewardship Guide

A resource center for pastors, ministries, and faith leaders to frame the conversation around biblical stewardship rather than worldly panic.

Feature: A theological resource center for pastors, ministries, and faith leaders to frame the data center discussion around biblical stewardship rather than worldly panic.

The "Golden Calf" Case Study Exodus 32

A study on the dangers of collective panic and industrial-scale FOMO, reminding communities not to sacrifice their actual, tangible heritage for a gleaming, temporary illusion.

While Moses lingers on the mountain, the people grow afraid that they have been left behind. They take their gold — their inheritance from Egypt, the wealth that was supposed to fund the long journey home — and they melt it down into a calf they can see and touch right now. The calf is not chosen because it is wise. It is chosen because the waiting felt unbearable.

The temptation to a town facing a hyperscale promise is the same. We are told if we do not vote yes this week, the project will go elsewhere. The inheritance — the quiet streets, the water table, the night-time peace — is offered up to be melted down into something shiny.

Stewardship in this passage is the courage to wait. The calf is always offered in the hour the prophet seems delayed. The leader's task is not to outshine the calf; it is to remind the people their actual inheritance is already in their hands.

The Climax of Job Job 38–42

A guide on human intellectual humility, emphasizing that true wisdom means recognizing the limits of human processing power and respecting the natural world God spoke into motion.

After thirty-seven chapters of human argument, God finally answers — and the answer is a torrent of questions. Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth? Who shut up the sea with doors? Have you commanded the morning since your days began? Can you bind the chains of the Pleiades?

This is the most important passage in scripture on the question of computational and technological humility. The answer to a world that boasts of "intelligence" measured in megawatts is the same answer Job receives: stand at the rim of the canyon, count the stars you cannot bind, and acknowledge that wisdom is not the same as processing power.

A town that learns to say "we do not yet know enough" is a town in the posture of Job at the end of the book. That is not weakness — that is the beginning of actual understanding.

The Ambition of the Quiet Life 1 Thessalonians 4:11

A devotional section celebrating Christian contentment, proving that a city's success is measured by the peace, health, and quiet preservation of its families — not by how many megawatts of electricity it burns to chase a digital gold rush.

"Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life, to mind your own business, and to work with your own hands."

It is one of the strangest commands in the New Testament: ambition directed toward quietness. Paul tells the Thessalonians the highest aim is the opposite of louder, taller, faster, bigger — a life so well-ordered that it does not require the noise of the city to validate it.

A town measured by quietness is measured by things you cannot put on a brochure: the depth of a child's sleep on a Tuesday night, the sound of a porch conversation at sunset, the steady hum of a refrigerator that does not have to compete with a chiller plant three miles away. A city that loves its neighbors will defend their nighttime peace as fiercely as it defends its tax base.

The "Love Your Neighbor" Infrastructure Check Galatians 6:2

An economic ethics study showing why communities must force large corporations to bear 100% of their own infrastructure costs, ensuring that fixed-income neighbors and local ministries aren't burdened with higher baseline utility bills.

"Bear ye one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ."

Read alongside Galatians 6:5 — "For every man shall bear his own burden" — this becomes one of the most useful passages in all of scripture for thinking about utility rate design. Verse 2 names the moral obligation of the strong to come alongside the weak. Verse 5 names the moral obligation of every actor — including the largest — to carry the weight of their own choices.

When a corporation chooses to build a facility that requires a new substation, new transmission lines, and new pumping capacity, those are that corporation's burdens to bear. The day that cost is silently spread onto the bills of a widow on social security or a small congregation paying for the sanctuary lights, the order of Galatians 6 is reversed.

A stewardship-minded city writes its zoning ordinance and its developer agreement in language that keeps verses 2 and 5 in their proper order. That is the simplest possible application of "love thy neighbor" to a kilowatt-hour bill.
